# Wheel Park ROI Calculator — Methodology & Configuration Reference **Version:** Current **Purpose:** Municipal investment advocacy for public skateparks, pump tracks, and combined wheel parks **Audience:** City councils, parks and recreation departments, municipal planners, community advocates --- ## Overview The Wheel Park ROI Calculator estimates the annual economic return of a publicly funded wheeled sport facility. It produces two distinct economic value estimates — one anchored to peer-reviewed academic research, one derived from proxy data with documented limitations — that together form a defensible floor-level investment case. **What the output represents:** Both pillars estimate *community economic value*, not municipal tax receipts, not direct facility revenue, and not return on investment in a commercial sense. The "value recovery" metric shows how long it takes for cumulative community economic benefit to equal the facility's construction cost. These figures are appropriate for public-good infrastructure framing before a council — they are not cash flow projections. **Conservative positioning:** All estimates are intentionally calibrated toward the lower end of what the supporting literature could justify. Advocates should present these as conservative floor estimates, not ceiling claims. --- ## Pillar 1 — Direct User Economic Surplus ### What it measures The economic value that facility users derive from visiting, measured as their willingness to pay above and beyond their actual travel costs. This is distinct from visitor spending (Pillar 2) — it is a measure of *benefit received*, not *dollars circulated*. ### Formula ``` P1 = Construction Cost × Annual Surplus Rate ``` ### Derivation P1 is anchored to the only peer-reviewed Travel Cost Model (TCM) study of a public skatepark facility: > Kemp, J. (2025). Estimating the user benefits associated with large public skateparks. > *Journal of Economic Analysis*, 4(1), 66–82. > https://www.anserpress.org/journal/jea/4/1/90 **Kemp (2025) baseline — Lauridsen Skatepark, Des Moines, Iowa:** - Facility size: 88,000 sq ft (largest public skatepark in the United States at time of study) - Estimated construction cost: approximately $6.2 million - Annual consumer surplus (adults): $488,000 - Per-adult-user-day benefit: $61 - Implied annual surplus rate: $488,000 ÷ $6,200,000 = **7.9% per year** The Travel Cost Model establishes user benefit by analyzing the relationship between a visitor's travel distance and frequency of visits. Users who travel further incur higher costs but still choose to visit, revealing their willingness to pay. The $61 figure is a TCM-derived consumer surplus — not an admission fee, but a revealed-preference measure of value received per adult user-day. ### Scaling logic The Lauridsen is an exceptional facility: the largest of its kind in the country, generating national destination draw. Smaller or less destination-oriented facilities produce proportionally lower surplus per construction dollar because: 1. Most of their users travel short distances, generating near-zero TCM-measured travel-cost surplus 2. Less unique or specialized facilities attract fewer willing-to-travel visitors Annual surplus rates applied in the calculator range from 3.0% to 7.2%, all set at or below the Lauridsen's 7.9% baseline. The destination skatepark rate of 7.2% is set slightly below the Lauridsen to reflect that even large regional destination skateparks are unlikely to match that facility's exceptional national profile. **P1 is driven by construction cost, not daily user count.** The "estimated daily users" input field does not affect Pillar 1. Changing that field only affects Pillar 2. ### Limitation The Kemp (2025) Lauridsen study is the *only* peer-reviewed TCM analysis of a public skatepark. Applying its framework to pump tracks and wheel parks requires analogy by facility type — no direct TCM study of pump tracks exists. This should be acknowledged when presenting P1 for non-skatepark configurations. --- ## Pillar 2 — Visitor & Tourism Spending ### What it measures The economic activity generated in the local economy by out-of-town visitors whose trip is primarily or partly motivated by the facility. This is expressed in direct community dollars — the actual spending on gas, food, lodging, and retail by non-local visitors, amplified by a standard economic multiplier. ### Formula ``` P2 = Daily Users × Visitor Fraction × Operating Days × Daily Visitor Spend × Economic Multiplier (1.72×) ``` ### Source data No peer-reviewed visitor spending study exists for public skateparks or pump tracks. P2 uses mountain bike tourism proxy data as the nearest available benchmark: - Whistler Bikepark visitor studies: $94–$133 per visitor per day - Northwest Arkansas cycling tourism study: $159.4 million annual economic impact from bicycle tourism; $416 average spending per destination trip (Trust for Public Land, 2025) - Economic multiplier: $1.72 per $1 of direct outdoor recreation visitor spending (Outdoor Industry Association, 2022) These are structurally similar facilities (destination-grade active recreation, purpose-driven travel), but they reflect a higher-income participant population than skateboarding. A downward income adjustment is applied as described below. ### Visitor fraction rationale Visitor fractions range from 1.5% (small neighborhood pump track) to 4.0% (destination skatepark). These are intentionally conservative. For comparison, major MTB trail networks routinely report 30–60% of users as out-of-town visitors. Wheeled sport facilities are generally not the sole driver of travel in the way that a Whistler or Moab destination is; the fractions here reflect a more realistic subset of daily users making purpose-driven trips from outside the local area. ### Income adjustment — why pump tracks and skateparks use different spend values A documented income differential exists between mountain bike and skateboarding participant populations: **Mountain biking (higher income):** Over 50% of mountain bike participants in the United States come from households earning $75,000 or more per year. The high cost of entry — capable trail bikes range from $2,000 to $8,000+ — creates a natural income filter. > Sources: > - SHRED TRAIL / OIA Outdoor Participation Survey data: > https://shredtrail.com/mountain-biking-statistics/ > - Singletracks.com reader demographics study (households earning $100K+ over-represented vs. U.S. population) > - American Trails, "Mountain Bicycling in Wisconsin: Surveyed and Analyzed" (54%+ of respondents earning above $50K, higher than local demographic baseline): > https://www.americantrails.org/resources/mountain-bicycling-in-wisconsin-surveyed-and-analyzed **Skateboarding (near national median):** Skateboarding participant household income is near or slightly below the national median (~$54,000), distributed broadly across income brackets rather than concentrated at the upper end. Skateboarding ranks in the bottom 10 of all tracked recreational activities by participant median household income. > Sources: > - National Sporting Goods Association (NSGA) sports participation by household income survey, summarized in: > Sports Destinations Management (2013). "Connecting Sports Participation with Household Income." > https://www.sportsdestinations.com/management/economics/nsga-study-8221 > - Tony Hawk Foundation / ERIC (2020). *Beyond the Board: Findings from the Field.* > Survey of 5,717 respondents; financial issues cited as top concern by 74% of skaters under 26. > https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED609263.pdf **Application in the calculator:** Because visitor spending scales with disposable income, pump track visitor spend estimates are set *above* the MTB proxy mid-range to reflect the MTB-family demographic that pump tracks primarily attract, while skatepark estimates are reduced below the proxy range to reflect the broader and lower-income skateboarding demographic. Combined wheel park figures split the difference, reflecting their mixed-discipline audience. The MTB proxy range from source studies is $94–$133 per visitor per day. Spend values in the calculator range from $20 (neighborhood skatepark) to $85 (destination pump track), representing substantial discounts from that range at all tiers. ### Operating days Default is 300 days per year. The slider allows adjustment from 180 to 365 days in 5-day increments to reflect local climate: | Days | Climate context | |---|---| | 340–365 | Mild (GA, TX, CA, FL) | | 300–339 | Temperate (CO, PA, OR, NC) | | 260–299 | Northern (MN, WI, NY, WA) | | 180–259 | Cold / harsh winters (ND, MT, AK) | --- ## Configuration Parameters — All 9 Presets The table below lists every parameter for each facility type and scale combination. Users arrive on foot, by bike, or by short car trip. Very low visitor fraction; near-zero travel-cost surplus in TCM terms. Benchmark construction costs reflect modest asphalt pump tracks ($50K–$120K) or small concrete skateparks ($150K–$400K). **Regional:** Serves a multi-town or multi-county area. Meaningful destination draw from surrounding communities; a mix of local daily users and regional day-trip visitors. Benchmark costs reflect mid-size asphalt pump tracks ($150K–$300K) or 10,000–15,000 sq ft concrete skateparks ($450K–$750K). **Destination:** Purpose-driven travel from a broad geographic region. Competition-grade or landmark facilities that function as a primary reason for travel. Visitor spend approaches the MTB proxy benchmark range. Benchmark costs: $350K–$600K for competition-grade pump tracks; $900K–$1.5M for large destination skateparks. --- ## Default Value Recovery — Reference Table The following payback estimates assume both pillars are active and operating days are set to 300. Expected daily users: 80. Climate is temperate (300 operating days). **P1 — Direct User Economic Surplus:** - Formula: $550,000 × 5.5% = **$30,250/year** - Interpretation: The community receives $30,250 annually in user economic surplus, equivalent to 5.5% of the capital invested, anchored to the Kemp (2025) Lauridsen baseline. **P2 — Visitor & Tourism Spending:** - Visitor-days: 80 users × 3.0% visitor fraction × 300 days = 720 visitor-days/year - Gross spend: 720 × $48/day = $34,560 - With multiplier: $34,560 × 1.72 = **$59,443/year** - Interpretation: Out-of-town visitors generate approximately $59,000 in annual local economic activity. Based on mountain bike tourism proxy data, adjusted downward for skateboarding demographics. **Combined:** - Total annual value: $30,250 + $59,443 = **$89,693/year** - Value recovery: $550,000 ÷ $89,693 = **6.1 years** - Annual return on construction cost: 16% --- ## Complete Source List **Primary study — P1 anchor:** Kemp, J. Results should be presented alongside original source studies. This tool does not constitute a certified economic analysis, engineering assessment, or legal or financial recommendation. The calculator and this methodology document were produced for public advocacy purposes. The underlying Kemp (2025) study is the only peer-reviewed economic analysis specific to skateparks; all other estimates involve extrapolation, proxy data, or analogy by facility type. Confidence levels are indicated within the calculator interface.
